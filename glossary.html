Glossary

A working vocabulary in Arabic.

A short, sourced glossary. These are the words a mathematician writing in Arabic used to name the objects and moves at the centre of algebra, arithmetic and geometry, the vocabulary that shaped the treatises in the Historical Profiles, and that quietly reappears, transliterated, in the words teachers still use today.

This introductory glossary draws every entry from a primary text, scholarly translation, or established historical reference. Where historians offer different interpretations, the entry presents the disagreement and identifies its sources.

Arabic reads from right to left. Transliteration follows the light academic convention used by MacTutor and the Encyclopaedia of Islam: long vowels marked with a macron, ʿ for ʿayn, ʾ for the glottal stop.

Twelve terms

Words at the centre of the tradition.

الجبر

al-jabr

Completion, restoration. The operation of moving a subtracted term to the other side of an equation to make it positive, adding the same quantity to both sides so nothing negative remains. One of the two moves in the title of al-Khwārizmī's al-Kitāb al-mukhtaṣar fī ḥisāb al-jabr wa'l-muqābala, and the ultimate source of the English word algebra.

Sources: MacTutor; Wikipedia, Al-Jabr; Etymonline.

المقابلة

al-muqābala

Balancing, confrontation. The second operation in al-Khwārizmī's title, cancelling like terms that appear on both sides of an equation, or subtracting equal quantities from both sides. Together with al-jabr it defines the reduction procedure that turns any quadratic into one of six standard forms.

Sources: Wikipedia, Al-Jabr; MacTutor.

حساب

ḥisāb

Calculation, arithmetic, reckoning. The general word for computational mathematics in Arabic. It appears in the full title of al-Khwārizmī's algebra treatise (fī ḥisāb, "in the calculation of") and in the name of many later works, including al-Karajī's al-Kāfī fī'l-ḥisāb ("The Sufficient on Calculation").

Sources: Wikipedia, Al-Jabr; MacTutor, al-Karajī.

هندسة

handasa

Geometry; engineering. The Arabic word for geometry, borrowed from Middle Persian handāz- ("to measure, allot, reckon"). In classical usage handasa covered both the pure discipline of geometry, as in Ibn al-Haytham's optical proofs, and the practical arts of measurement and construction.

Source: Wiktionary, Arabic roots ه ن د س.

جذر

jidhr

Root. The unknown quantity x in al-Khwārizmī's algebra, literally the botanical root, from which the "root of an equation" gets its name in every European language. Contemporary sources sometimes use shayʾ ("thing") for the same idea; both terms appear side by side in early Arabic algebra.

Sources: Muslim Heritage, Al-Khwarizmi; Katz, Episodes in the Mathematics of Medieval Islam (excerpt, U. Kentucky).

مال

māl

Wealth, possession, the square of the unknown. Literally "wealth" or "an amount of money", but in algebra the square of the root, i.e. x2. Historians of mathematics note the double meaning is deliberate: early Arabic algebra grew out of arithmetical problems about amounts of money and their square roots, and the older meaning survives inside the algebraic one.

Sources: Oaks & Alkhateeb, "A Conceptual Analysis of Early Arabic Algebra" (CiteSeerX); Muslim Heritage.

شيء

shayʾ

Thing, an unknown. The other Arabic word for the unknown quantity in an equation, alongside jidhr. Translated into medieval Latin as res ("thing") and into Italian as cosa, giving rise to the Renaissance term for early symbolic algebra: l'arte della cosa, the "art of the thing".

Source: Oaks, "Algebraic Symbolism in Medieval Arabic Algebra" (PhilArchive).

كعب

kaʿb · mukaʿʿab (مكعب)

Cube. The third power of the unknown, x3, and the geometrical cube. Sesiano's edition of a tenth century Arabic algebraic compendium gives the standard sequence: jidhr (root, x), māl (square, x2), then mukaʿʿab, "the product of the last two". The word itself is related to kaʿb ("heel, ankle bone, die"), the shape of ancient dice cut from animal bones.

Sources: Sesiano, "An Arabic Algebraic Compendium of 1000 CE" (SCIAMVS); Cambridge Mathematics, "Down on their luck".

عدد

ʿadad

Number. The general word for a numerical quantity. In al-Khwārizmī's algebra, the equations are built from three kinds of objects: units (ʿadad), roots (jidhr) and squares (māl). Plural aʿdād (أعداد).

Source: Sesiano, "An Arabic Algebraic Compendium of 1000 CE" (SCIAMVS).

زاوية

zāwiya

Angle; corner. The Arabic word for a geometric angle, from the root z-w-y ("to draw together, gather"). Modern usage keeps the mathematical senses right next to the everyday one, zāwiya ḥādda is an acute angle, zāwiya qāʾima is a right angle, and a zāwiya is also just the corner of a room.

Sources: Wiktionary, زاوية; Lane's Lexicon.

دائرة

dāʾira

Circle. From the verb dāra ("to revolve, turn around"), literally "that which turns". The word describes a closed circuit in motion, fitting the Greek geometric tradition extended by al-Bīrūnī, Ibn al-Haytham, and others.

Source: Wiktionary, دائرة.

مثلث

muthallath

Triangle. Built on the root th l th ("three"), the word literally means "a shape with three sides". The pattern extends to murabbaʿ (مربع, quadrilateral, from rubʿ, "four"), musaddas (مسدس, hexagon), and muthamman (مثمن, octagon). Arabic mathematical vocabulary makes the numeric structure of shapes unusually clear.

Source: Wiktionary, مثلث.

This glossary will grow as the Academy publishes more archive material. Terms are chosen for their value during a lesson. The full vocabulary of medieval Arabic mathematics is far larger.
