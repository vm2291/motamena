Al-Khwārizmī

محمد بن موسى الخوارزمي

Muḥammad ibn Mūsā al-Khwārizmī

c. 780 to c. 850 · Baghdad

Al-Khwārizmī worked in Baghdad in the first half of the ninth century, under the patronage of the caliph al-Ma'mūn. MacTutor and Britannica both place his life span at approximately 780 to 850, and both identify Baghdad as his primary place of work, though the association of his name with the region of Khwārazm, south of the Aral Sea, is also discussed as a possible indicator of origin. Neither source gives exact birth or death dates.

Principal treatises

His most important work is the algebra treatise Al-Kitāb al-mukhtaṣar fī ḥisāb al-jabr wa'l-muqābala الكتاب المختصر في حساب الجبر والمقابلة , rendered by MacTutor as Hisab al-jabr w'al-muqabala, and often translated as The Compendious Book on Calculation by Completion and Balancing. The English word "algebra" derives from al-jabr, one of the two operations named in the title. The other three works attributable to him are:

  • A Latin translation known as Algoritmi de numero Indorum on Hindu Arabic decimal place value numerals and arithmetic. The Arabic original is lost, and the word "algorithm" derives from the Latinised form of his name in this title.
  • Kitāb ṣūrat al-arḍ كتاب صورة الأرض, a geography giving coordinates for over two thousand localities, building on and revising Ptolemy's Geography.
  • A zīj (a set of astronomical tables) drawing on Indian and Greek sources, including a sine table.

The mathematics that matters to a MOTA classroom

The algebra treatise's core move is a classification. Al-Khwārizmī reduces any linear or quadratic equation to one of six standard forms, squares equal to roots; squares equal to numbers; roots equal to numbers; squares and roots equal to numbers; squares and numbers equal to roots; roots and numbers equal to squares, and then handles each form individually, giving both an algebraic solution method and a geometric justification for it. The equation MacTutor uses as its worked example, \(x^2 + 10x = 39\), is solved by a completing the square argument that literally dissects a geometric square. The treatise closes with a section applying these methods to problems in inheritance law.

For a student preparing for an olympiad, the classification is the central move. A whole family of problems can be reduced to a small number of standard shapes, and that each shape can be handled by a documented method backed by geometric proof, is the same instinct behind every good problem solving taxonomy taught in the Academy's algebra strand.

On the label "father of algebra"

MacTutor reports Solomon Gandz's view that Al-Khwārizmī is "more entitled to be called 'the father of algebra' than Diophantus, because al-Khwarizmi is the first to teach algebra in an elementary form and for its own sake." Other historians disagree or qualify this framing. Roshdi Rashed's D'al-Khwārizmī à Descartes presents a related but distinct argument about the birth of algebraic thinking in this period. We present the debate as MacTutor does: a named scholarly position within an active discussion. The treatise is foundational.
