Historical Profiles · Entry 04 / 12

Abū al-Wafāʾ al-Būzjānī

أبو الوفاء محمد بن محمد بن يحيى بن إسماعيل بن العباس البوزجاني

Abū al-Wafāʾ Muḥammad ibn Muḥammad ibn Yaḥyā ibn Ismāʿīl ibn al-ʿAbbās al-Būzjānī

10 June 940 to 15 July 998 · Buzjān (Khorāsān) → Baghdad

Al-Būzjānī was born on 10 June 940 in Buzjān, a small town near Jām in the Khorāsān region (modern Iran), and died on 15 July 998 in Baghdad. He moved to Baghdad in 959, joining the court of the Buyid ruler ʿAḍud al-Dawla, and remained in the city for the rest of his life. Late in that career, under ʿAḍud al-Dawla's son Sharaf al-Dawla, an observatory was set up in a Baghdad palace garden and opened in June 988; its instruments included a wall quadrant more than six metres long and a stone sextant eighteen metres across.

Principal works

  • Kitāb fī mā yaḥtāj ilayh al-kuttāb wa-l-ʿummāl min ʿilm al-ḥisāb (Book on What Is Necessary from the Science of Arithmetic for Scribes and Businessmen), a practical arithmetic manual, written between 961 and 976. Britannica notes it as one of two surviving practical treatises.
  • Kitāb fī mā yaḥtāj ilayh al-ṣāniʿ min al-aʿmāl al-handasiyya (Book on What Is Necessary from Geometric Constructions for the Artisan), written after 990, in thirteen chapters, on the design and testing of drafting instruments, right angle construction, approximate angle trisections, parabola constructions, regular polygons and their inscription in and circumscription about circles, the division of plane polygons, and the division of spherical surfaces into regular spherical polygons.
  • Kitāb al-Kāmil, his major astronomical treatise, following the Almagest in structure while simplifying and improving its calculations.
  • Theories of the Moon, the treatise in which his lunar trigonometry is worked out.
  • Commentaries and translations, now lost, on Euclid, Diophantus, and al-Khwārizmī.

The trigonometric tables

Al-Būzjānī compiled tables of sines and tangents at 15 arcminute intervals , that is, at quarter degree resolution, accurate to the equivalent of eight decimal places (MacTutor's phrasing, converted from the sexagesimal of the original). The point of comparison: Ptolemy's tables were accurate to three decimal places. He devised a new method for calculating the sine table, and, in his lunar work, he introduced and tabulated the secant and cosecant alongside the tangent and cotangent. Britannica adds a specific hedge that we preserve: the lunar inequality later called variation belongs to a different attribution.

The tangent function, the careful version of the claim

MacTutor summarises al-Būzjānī's most cited contribution as the first use of the tan function and compiling tables of sines and tangents at 15′ intervals. Britannica describes him more cautiously as having utilized all six trigonometric functions and calculated tables for them, anticipating modern usage by suggesting the unit circle as the definition.

We flag one hedge: some historians of trigonometry credit the tangent function instead to al-Marwazī (Ḥabash al-Ḥāsib), an earlier ninth century figure. The Wikipedia summary of al-Būzjānī notes this alternative attribution explicitly. The historical record firmly supports al-Būzjānī's systematic tabulation of the tangent and his treatment of it as a central function within a complete system of six functions, the framing that a modern student of trigonometry inherits.

The spherical law of sines, four candidates

The spherical law of sines,

\[ \frac{\sin A}{\sin a} = \frac{\sin B}{\sin b} = \frac{\sin C}{\sin c}, \]

was discovered in the tenth century by a mathematician working in the Arab Persian tradition. The original attribution remains contested. According to D'Ambrosio and Selin, the discovery is variously attributed to Abū Maḥmūd al-Khujandī, Abū al-Wafāʾ al-Būzjānī, Abū Naṣr Manṣūr ibn ʿIrāq, and Naṣīr al-Dīn al-Ṭūsī. Glen Van Brummelen (Heavenly Mathematics) narrows the field to al-Būzjānī or Abū Naṣr Manṣūr. MacTutor's biography of Abū Naṣr Manṣūr states plainly that Abu'l-Wafa may have discovered this law first and Abu Nasr Mansur may have learnt it from him. Britannica's al-Būzjānī entry states that he proved the law of sines for general spherical triangles. Britannica leaves priority open. Our account follows that position: al-Būzjānī gave a rigorous proof of the spherical law of sines; whether the theorem is originally his is still open.

Constructions with a fixed compass

A distinctive strand of al-Būzjānī's Geometric Constructions is its treatment of problems solvable with straightedge and a compass of fixed radius, sometimes called a rusty compass. MacTutor: There are a whole collection of problems which he solves using a ruler and fixed compass, that is one where the angle between the legs of the compass is fixed. The motivation was practical: in practice they give more exact results than can be obtained by changing the compass opening. For an olympiad reader, this is a live constraint, problems on fixed compass constructibility, or on constructions with a limited toolset, still surface in competition mathematics, and al-Būzjānī's book is where much of that tradition begins.

Related curriculum links in the curriculum

Al-Būzjānī's trigonometry sits directly between Al-Battānī's shift from chord to sine and Al-Bīrūnī's trigonometric geodesy. His constructive geometry, meanwhile, opens the door to a tradition of geometric problem solving under constraint that runs through olympiad geometry to this day. The Continuum places him inside a tenth century Baghdad cluster (al-Battānī on one side, al-Bīrūnī on the other); the About page's Heritage section gives the wider institutional framing.
