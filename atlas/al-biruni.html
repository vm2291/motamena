Al-Bīrūnī

أبو الريحان محمد بن أحمد البيروني

Abū al-Rayḥān Muḥammad ibn Aḥmad al-Bīrūnī

15 September 973 to 13 December 1048 · Khwārazm → Ghazna, with fieldwork in India

Al-Bīrūnī was born on 15 September 973 in Kāth, in Khwārazm (in modern Karakalpakstan, Uzbekistan), and died on 13 December 1048 in Ghazna (modern Ghazni, Afghanistan). His career spanned an unusual number of geographies: Kāth, Jurjāniyya, Rayy, Gīlān, Baghdad, Gurgān, Kabul, and extended fieldwork in northern India, the Punjab, and near Kashmir. MacTutor's biography documents specific observations he made in each place, dated to the day.

Principal works

  • Chronology of Ancient Nations, dedicated around the year 1000. In it Al-Bīrūnī refers to seven of his own earlier works, spanning the decimal system, the astrolabe, astronomical observation, astrology, and history.
  • India, his most famous work; a study of Indian religion, philosophy, caste structure, numeral systems, geography, astronomy, and calendar systems, based on direct study of Sanskrit sources.
  • Al-Qānūn al-Masʿūdī (The Masudic Canon), contains a table of coordinates for roughly 600 locations. MacTutor attributes some measurements to Al-Bīrūnī and others to a table by Al-Khwārizmī.
  • Shadows, on gnomonics, and containing a history of the tangent and secant functions.
  • Taḥdīd, the work in which he reports on al-Khujandī's large sextant and its observational limitations.
  • A short surviving work on map projections, including one of his own devising.

The Earth radius measurement

Al-Bīrūnī's best known geodetic result: using a trigonometric measurement made from a mountaintop, he calculated the Earth's radius as 6339.6 km. MacTutor characterises this specifically as "a value not obtained in the West until the 16th century." The calculation matters for the method it reveals. Al-Bīrūnī reduced a geographic scale measurement to a tractable trigonometric problem, worked it carefully, and reported his result, and reported his uncertainty about it, in a way directly recognisable to a modern student of experimental technique.

The mathematics MacTutor lists

MacTutor's summary of Al-Bīrūnī's mathematics lists trigonometry (including the plane and spherical sine theorem, which Al-Bīrūnī himself attributed to his teacher Abū Naṣr Manṣūr, in a letter to al-Sijzī), geometry (conic sections, angle trisection, stereographic projection), summation of series, ratio theory, and "combinatorial analysis" as an explicit topic heading. The extracted text presents combinatorial analysis as a topic entry. We therefore describe the documented range of his work and reserve specific breakthrough claims for evidence with fuller detail.

An honest characterisation

MacTutor's own summary judgement is that Al-Bīrūnī was "not a great innovator of original theories, mathematical or otherwise, but a careful observer." We preserve that assessment as written. His work offers a MOTA classroom a model of rigorous measurement, honest error reporting, and the intellectual honesty of attributing a result to the correct predecessor, as he did with the sine theorem through an explicit credit to his teacher.

Related curriculum links in the curriculum

Al-Bīrūnī's work touches geometry (trigonometry, projections, conic sections) and the broader habit of extremal problem solving that runs through olympiad training. The shorter Heritage narrative on the About page places him alongside his contemporaries.
