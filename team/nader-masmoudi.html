Team · Founder & Principal Investigator

Dr. Nader Masmoudi

نادر المصمودي

Nāder al-Maṣmūdī

b. 1974, Sfax · NYU Courant · PI, SITE at NYU Abu Dhabi

Nader Masmoudi was born in Sfax, Tunisia, in 1974. He is a mathematician whose subject is the mathematics of fluids, how they move, when they hold their shape, and when they stop being predictable. Over the last quarter century he has become one of the small number of analysts who can hold the whole of that question in view: the Euler and Navier Stokes equations, boundary layers in the sense of Prandtl, the long time behaviour of shear flows, the passage from microscopic kinetic descriptions to continuum ones. He is a Professor at the Courant Institute of Mathematical Sciences at New York University, and an affiliated faculty member at NYU Abu Dhabi.

Sfax to Moscow

He came out of the Tunisian school system, and by his last year of it was already known. In July 1992 he travelled to Moscow for the International Mathematical Olympiad and won gold, the first African and the first Arab contestant ever to do so. The official record has him at rank 14 in a field of 351 competitors, with a score of 34 out of 42 and full marks on the first four problems. Tunisia and the result remains unmatched in Tunisia.

He has spoken elsewhere about who he read as a schoolboy: This is who I looked up to while I was young, these were some of my idols. The figures he names are al-Khwārizmī and Omar Khayyām. It is worth pausing on the fact that a Tunisian teenager preparing for the world’s hardest mathematics contest was reading algebra from the ninth and eleventh centuries as a living inheritance.

Paris

He moved to France for the classes préparatoires at the Lycée Louis-le-Grand. In 1994 he came first in the joint entrance competition of the École Normale Supérieure and the École Polytechnique, and was awarded the Prize of the Republic of Tunisia for academic achievement. His diploma at ENS Paris followed in 1996. He then went to Paris-Dauphine, where he took his doctorate in 1999 under the direction of Pierre-Louis Lions, with a thesis titled Problèmes asymptotiques en mécanique des fluides. His Habilitation à Diriger des Recherches, on fluid mechanics and gas dynamics, was completed at the same university in 2000. From 1998 through 2000 he held a research position at the CNRS in Paris.

Courant

He moved to the Courant Institute after his doctorate. He has been a Professor of Mathematics there since 2008 and has stayed. His office is in Warren Weaver Hall. The work has kept close to a single organising question: when does a flow described by a nonlinear partial differential equation remain close to a simple reference state, and which conditions cause it to depart. The answer is rarely soft. A small perturbation of a shear flow may damp or form structures beyond the reach of linear theory. A boundary layer may stay thin and orderly or separate. He has spent his career making those distinctions rigorous.

The result that many analysts point to first is his 2013 proof, with his then postdoctoral fellow Jacob Bedrossian, of the nonlinear inviscid damping and asymptotic stability of the two dimensional Couette flow for the Euler equations. The problem had been open in a serious sense since Kelvin's linear stability argument of 1887 and Orr's of 1907. Their proof used ideas from Cédric Villani's work on Landau damping in plasma physics, the two subjects turned out to share a structure. He described it plainly in a talk titled The Two Brothers: Landau damping and inviscid damping, given at the 2014 Fields Medal Symposium held in Cédric Villani's honour at the Fields Institute in Toronto. Related joint work addressed the viscous case, and the formation and stability of Prandtl type boundary layers in the two dimensional Navier Stokes equations.

What he works on

  • Nonlinear partial differential equations of hydrodynamics: the Euler and Navier Stokes equations, surface, gravity, capillary and acoustic waves, and the qualitative behaviour of boundary layers.
  • Boundary layer theory in the Prandtl regime, including well posedness and separation.
  • Hydrodynamic stability, in particular the long time behaviour of Couette flow and other shear flows, and the phenomenon of inviscid damping.
  • Landau damping and the nonlinear stability of equilibria in kinetic models of plasmas.
  • Hydrodynamic limits of the Boltzmann equation, and the passage from compressible to incompressible regimes.
  • Nonlinear Schrödinger equations, Ginzburg Landau equations, and the Keller Segel model of chemotaxis.

His long collaborative record includes Pierre-Louis Lions, Jean-Yves Chemin, Laure Saint-Raymond, Pierre Germain, Jalal Shatah, Emmanuel Grenier and Jacob Bedrossian, among others.

How he talks about the subject

He presents mathematics as a shared discipline with broad connections. In a 2019 interview he described stability as a word that we use in our daily life, not only in math and physics, it applies to social science, politics, or even relationships. What’s interesting is the kind of questions we ask in math or physics, you can ask in social sciences, political sciences, or even relationships. He said much the same about interdisciplinary work: my office is located right next to a professor of psychology and we often discuss how our fields connect. I am always open to interdisciplinary application.

On why more young people in the region need to enter research mathematics, he was direct: It’s true that Arabs usually think about being engineers or doing this kind of academic research, so research in general is no longer in the habit, but if we go back to Muslim mathematicians in the past they were in forefront of the field.

And on why the pure end of mathematics is worth defending on its own terms: Math is like a language, whereas even today we try to distinguish between pure math and applied math. Sometimes pure math is being done without any application in mind, but it turns out that some pure math done from long ago we have only begun to understand their application now. He gave the standard example, nineteenth century number theory becoming the basis for modern cryptography, and then this: You never know. Something that has no result other than to be math today can become tomorrow’s solution.

Distinctions

  • 1992, Gold Medal, International Mathematical Olympiad, Moscow. First African and first Arab gold medallist.
  • 1994, Ranked first in the joint entrance competition of the École Normale Supérieure and the École Polytechnique; Prize of the Republic of Tunisia.
  • 2011, Best scientific article, Annales Henri Poincaré.
  • 2012, National Science Foundation Fellowship.
  • 2017, Fermat Prize, Institut de Mathématiques de Toulouse. Cited by the jury for his very deep and creative work on nonlinear partial differential equations.
  • 2018, Invited speaker, International Congress of Mathematicians, Rio de Janeiro (Partial Differential Equations).
  • 2021, Elected Fellow of the American Academy of Arts and Sciences.
  • 2022, King Faisal Prize in Science (Mathematics), awarded jointly with Martin Hairer, for work on Euler and boundary layer equations that reset earlier assumptions about the range of validity of the classical models.

At NYU Abu Dhabi

At NYU Abu Dhabi he founded and leads the Center for Stability, Instability and Turbulence, a research centre within the NYUAD Research Institute where he serves as Principal Investigator. He chairs the Math Olympiad Trainer Academy, which sits inside that centre. That’s the shortest true summary; the longer version of the Academy’s founding logic lives on the About page.
